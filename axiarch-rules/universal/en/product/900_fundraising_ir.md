## Part I. Fundraising Philosophy

### 1.1. Three Fundraising Mistakes

- **Rule 15.001**: Define the following mindsets as dangerous anti-patterns in fundraising

| Anti-Pattern | Problem |
|:------------|:--------|
| **Fundraising as Validation** | Mistaking the raise amount for proof of success; VC money does not replace PMF |
| **Raise as Much as Possible** | Excessive capital increases dilution and erodes operational discipline |
| **Valuation Maximization** | Inflated valuations make the next round and exit more difficult (Down Round risk) |

### 1.2. Default Alive Principle

- **Rule 15.002**: Strive to be **Default Alive** before raising (Paul Graham)
  - Default Alive = At current growth rate and cost structure, the business will naturally reach profitability
  - When raising without being Default Alive, explicitly understand and acknowledge the risk

### 1.3. Ramen Profitability First

- **Rule 15.003**: Prioritize reaching Ramen Profitability (minimum viable revenue to sustain founders) early
  - This creates a position of **strength in fundraising negotiations** (negotiating from desperation always leads to worse terms)

## Part II. Funding Stages & Timing

### 2.1. Stage-by-Stage Characteristics & Benchmarks

- **Rule 15.010**: Organize funding stages and corresponding achievement benchmarks in the following framework

| Stage | Raise Range | Achievement Benchmark | Primary Investors |
|:------|:-----------|:---------------------|:----------------|
| **Pre-seed** | < $300K | Idea + Team | Angels / FFF |
| **Seed** | $300K – $2M | Early PMF signals + MRR > $20K | Seed VCs / Angels |
| **Series A** | $2M – $15M | PMF confirmed + MRR > $80K + MoM 10%+ | Series A VCs |
| **Series B** | $15M – $50M | Unit economics proven + ARR > $8M | Growth VCs / CVCs |
| **Series C+** | $50M+ | Scale confirmed + market leadership | PE / Large VCs |

### 2.2. Fundraising Timing Law

- **Rule 15.011**: Judge fundraising timing by the following principles

```
Timing rules:
1. Start 12 months before you need it (process takes 2–3 months)
2. Begin when runway is > 12 months
3. Move when momentum is strong (your growth metrics are showing well)
4. Never negotiate from desperation — terms will be unfavorable

"Borrow money when you have too much, not when you don't have enough"
```

### 2.3. Equity vs. Debt vs. Bootstrap

- **Rule 15.012**: Select the financing structure using the following criteria

| Method | Best For | Risk |
|:-------|:---------|:-----|
| **Equity** | Rapid growth requiring a big bet | Dilution / shareholder accountability |
| **Debt (Loans)** | Stable cash flow | Repayment obligation / collateral |
| **Revenue-based** | Stable SaaS MRR | Repayment terms need scrutiny |
| **Bootstrap** | High-margin niche / long-term ownership | Limited growth speed |

## Part III. Pitch Design Protocol

### 3.1. Pitch Deck Structure (Standard 15 Slides)

- **Rule 15.020**: Use the following as the standard pitch deck structure

```
Required Slides (10):
1. Cover (Company name, tagline, logo)
2. Problem (Problem definition and scope)
3. Solution (Product solution / demo)
4. Market Size (TAM / SAM / SOM)
5. Business Model (Revenue mechanics / pricing)
6. Traction (Metrics, KPIs, growth charts)
7. Go-to-Market (Acquisition strategy)
8. Competition (Competitive comparison / positioning)
9. Team (Founders and key members)
10. Financials (3-year projection, raise size, use of proceeds)

Appendix Slides (5):
11. Unit Economics (CAC / LTV / Magic Number)
12. Product Roadmap
13. Customer Testimonials / Case Studies
14. Technology / Architecture
15. Cap Table (Current ownership structure)
```

### 3.2. Traction Slide Design Principles

- **Rule 15.021**: The Traction slide is the most important slide

```
Good ways to show traction:
✅ Emphasize growth rate (MoM 20% = 792% annualized)
✅ Show logos (notable customer brand names)
✅ Combine qualitative + quantitative (NPS + MRR)
✅ Show customer success with before/after

Avoid:
❌ Absolute numbers only ("100K users" — context not clear)
❌ Vanity Metrics (pageviews / registration count only; show engagement)
❌ Cherry-picking time periods (selecting only high-growth windows)
```

### 3.3. Narrative Design

- **Rule 15.022**: Design the overall pitch narrative as a single, cohesive story

```
Recommended Narrative Structure:
1. The World is Changing (Why Now): A major technology or market shift is happening
2. The Problem: A problem created by / revealed by that shift
3. The Solution: How we solve it
4. The Proof (Traction): Evidence the market wants this
5. The Vision: The world we'll create in 5 years
```

## Part IV. Investor Selection & Outreach

### 4.1. Investor List Building Protocol

- **Rule 15.030**: Manage investor outreach with the following funnel discipline

```
Target: Full investor list (200+ firms)
  ↓ Research
Qualified: Matching stage/thesis (50 firms)
  ↓ Check for warm intro routes
Priority: Firms where warm intro exists (20 firms)
  ↓ Meetings
Meeting: First meeting completed (10 firms)
  ↓ Gauge interest
Active: Moving toward deeper engagement (5 firms)
  ↓ Term Sheet
Closing: Term sheets received (1–3 firms)
```

### 4.2. Warm Intro Importance

- **Rule 15.031**: Cold outreach to investors has < 1% response rate. **Always approach through warm introduction**
  - Best order: Portfolio founders > LPs > Other VCs > Advisors > Industry contacts
  - Cold outreach is a last resort when all intro channels are exhausted

### 4.3. Investor Fit Evaluation

- **Rule 15.032**: Evaluate investors along the following axes to find the "best partner"

| Axis | What to Verify |
|:-----|:-------------|
| **Thesis Fit** | Does the investment theme match your stage and domain? |
| **Portfolio Value-add** | Are there potential customers or partners in their portfolio? |
| **Founder Reputation** | Reference check portfolio founders for their experience with the investor |
| **Board Value** | What can the investor contribute as a board member? |
| **Co-investment** | Do they have a track record of following on in subsequent rounds? |

## Part V. Term Sheet & Negotiation

### 5.1. Key Term Sheet Conditions

- **Rule 15.040**: Evaluate the following conditions with legal counsel before agreeing

| Condition | Watch Out For |
|:----------|:-------------|
| **Valuation (Pre/Post-money)** | Option Pool Shuffle (VC-favorable dilution structure) |
| **Liquidation Preference** | 1x Non-participating is founder-friendly; ≥ 2x is concerning |
| **Anti-dilution** | Avoid Full Ratchet; Broad-based Weighted Average is standard |
| **Pro-rata Rights** | Investor right to maintain percentage in future rounds; acceptable |
| **Board Composition** | Ensure founders maintain board majority |
| **Information Rights** | Confirm scope of monthly/quarterly financial reporting obligations |

### 5.2. Red Flag Conditions List

- **Rule 15.041**: The following conditions require legal review as Red Flags

```
Red Flag Conditions:
❌ Participating Preferred Stock (double claim: liquidation preference + pro-rata residual participation)
❌ Weak founder protection without comprehensive Drag-along Rights
❌ Excessive investor control: consent rights over hiring/firing, veto over key decisions
❌ Aggressive Ratchet provisions (dilution linked to next round valuation)
❌ Over-broad ROFR (Right of First Refusal): restricts secondary sales
```

### 5.3. Competing Offers Strategy

- **Rule 15.042**: Obtaining term sheets from multiple investors simultaneously creates the most advantageous negotiating position
  - **FOMO Strategy**: Create a deadline ("We plan to decide by end of month given strong interest")
  - **Competitive Tension**: Ask a specific investor for their best terms framed as "we'll go with you if you match X"

## Part VI. Due Diligence Preparation

### 6.1. Data Room Structure

- **Rule 15.050**: Maintain the following documents in a Data Room at all times, ready for due diligence

```
Data Room Structure:
├── Corporate/ (Incorporation docs, cap table, board minutes)
├── Financials/ (Monthly P&L, BS, CF, budgets)
├── Product/ (Product Roadmap, architecture diagrams)
├── Legal/ (Contracts, IP rights, litigation risk)
├── HR/ (Org chart, employment agreements, stock option schedule)
├── Customers/ (Customer list, MRR breakdown, churn analysis)
├── Technology/ (Code quality, security posture)
└── Market/ (Market research, competitive analysis)
```

### 6.2. Due Diligence Principles

- **Rule 15.051**: Define the following information disclosure principles for DD
  - **Proactive Disclosure**: Disclose problems voluntarily. The damage when concealment is discovered later is always greater than upfront disclosure
  - **Consistency**: Provide all investors the same information; never create information asymmetry
  - **Completeness**: "I didn't know" is not acceptable. Fully understand your company's state before engaging

## Part VII. Investor Relations (IR)

### 7.1. Monthly Investor Report Standard Format

- **Rule 15.060**: Sending a monthly investor report is mandatory

```markdown
# [Monthly] Investor Report — YYYY MM

## TL;DR (3-line summary)
- WIN: [X] achieved / [X] customer acquired
- MISS: [X] missed / Reason and countermeasures
- ASK: What we're asking for this month

## Key Metrics
| Metric | This Month | Last Month | MoM |
| MRR    | $XM        | $XM        | +X% |
| Customers | X         | X          | +X% |

## Highlights (What went well)

## Lowlights (Challenges / Learnings)

## Help Needed (Requests to investors)
- Hiring: Looking for X engineer
- Introductions: Looking for customers in X industry
```

### 7.2. Board Meeting Operations Protocol

- **Rule 15.061**: Operate Board Meetings with the following protocol

```
Frequency: Monthly (pre-Series A) / Bi-monthly (post-Series A)
Board Pack Delivery: 72 hours before meeting
Structure:
  1. Performance review (KPIs / financials)
  2. Discussion of key challenges
  3. Items requiring decisions
  4. Next Actions and owners

Prohibited: "Surprises" in Board Meetings. Always pre-communicate major bad news
```

### 7.3. Investor Early Warning Protocol

- **Rule 15.062**: Notify all investors within 24 hours of the following material events

```
Events requiring Early Warning:
- Runway dropping below 6 months
- Key person departure (founder, CTO, etc.)
- Significant security incident
- Major customer churn (> 10% of MRR from one customer)
- Significant legal risk emerging
- Major competitor move (acquisition, large raise, etc.)
```
